Raising The Score

CARE: MALAWI
JANUARY 2015 - MAY 2015

CARE: Malawi, a branch of the World Health Organization, invited UNC's School of Media and Journalism to cover their efforts to improve maternal health care in rural Malawi.

A team of approximately 30 UNC students and professors traveled to Africa with hundreds of pounds of recording equipment and dozens of laptops in tow.

For one week, we stayed in the village of Ntcheu, following the lives of Malawians affected by the work of CARE. While half the team created video stories out in the field, the other half designed and developed a website and graphics to house those stories. I created the logo and design style guide—and illustrated the website's panorama—around which the aesthetic of the project molded.

Raising The Score was a lesson in collaboration with people of various skillsets, working remotely in abnormal and exotic conditions, and cooperating with an international client.

Planning Our Approach

Prior to pitching a concept to the rest of the team, the designers and developers needed to agree on a direction of presenting the stories and on the project's aesthetic.

I created this mockup image as a way of showing the concept for a parallax scrolling website. The background would be an illustrated scene of the Malawian landscape. A user would perceive depth by the different speeds at which the background content scrolled. Foreground, narrative content would shift in and out of view dynamically as the user navigated the panorama.

For the purpose of the mockup, details were excluded; this was designed solely for getting the point across to the other designers and developers. We approved the idea for pitching with the understanding it would be fleshed out further.

Pitch Part 1

After I refined the mockup, the designers and developers presented the idea in a question and answer format to the entire team. We gained a good perspective of what we needed to accomplish while in Malawi after fielding questions and receiving extensive feedback.

Pitch Part 2

We not only planned out the home screen panorama, but also planned the story pages and the navigation for presentation.

Pitch Part 3

The proposed typography and color choices—partially based on CARE's branding, while incorporating complementary organic colors.

Advanced preparations were made over a 2 month period in order to hit the ground running once we arrived in Africa.

The Style Guide

The purpose of the guide was to achieve graphical consistency within the team. I created this guide by taking inspiration from the first few days in Malawi, capturing the natural beauty of the landscape in the color choices.

I began creating assets for the final panorama and assisted my teammates in the art work of the graphics.

What Would Have Been

The project's original name and wordmark (created while in Africa) until two weeks before the deadline. "MAYI" means "mother" in Chichewa, the native tongue of Malawians. The silhouette depicts a Malawian mother carrying a child on her back—inspired from a photo taken in Ntcheu by my former professor, Steven King.

A Last Minute Improvement

About a month after returning from Africa, CARE Malawi requested a change in the name of the project to reflect their method of quantifying their work—the Scorecard. The new logo combined elements of the Malawian flag with the silhouette of the mother on the move—to symbolize the ongoing progress being made to improve maternal health care.

Website Panorama

As we were attempting the original concept, we realized the developers would not be able to achieve parallax scrolling, which would have given the panorama more depth.

Given the time constraints, I altered the design to the best of my ability, understanding the new parameters set by the developers. Our new intention was to scroll through the panorama as one flat image that would responsively adjust its size to the browser window.

The video scrolls through that panorama as I had designed it.

Ultimately, I was disappointed in the final execution of the website. Our team was not able to succeed in making the panorama responsive.

A number of factors led to this end: a lack of time, a lack of experience in designer/developer interaction, and a lack of experience in prioritization. However, the experience was vital in understanding how to approach these sorts of collaborative projects in the future.
