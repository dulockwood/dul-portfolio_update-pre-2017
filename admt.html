No Religion Is The New Religion

ADMiT | FREELANCE CLIENT
NOVEMBER 2016 - PRESENT

A good friend of mine, interested in taking his passion to the next level, requested my expertise to produce an album cover for his future EP. This project had fewer creative restraints, allowing me to craft a product unlike the results of other assignments.

A Work In Progress

This is the current iteration of the album cover. The client's artist name and the EP title have yet to be decided, hence the lack of typographic elements.

Although background detail and coloration is to my liking, I intend to rework the subject of the cover—it was originally drawn as a proof-of-concept and not meant to be the final artwork.

Brief

The direction was minimal, as seen in this excerpt of a Facebook conversation. It was enough to conceptualize the finished product.

Concept Sketch

I drew a quick manifestation of my ideas, which allowed me to figure out how to proceed and execute the concept.

The text on the left side of the image was retroactively added as a way of keeping track of Photoshop layers. The text on the right side of the image shows the hex values of the colors chosen after color exploration.

Execution

I began in Adobe Illustrator, tracing a photo of a man in the previously described pose. I created a symmetrical geometric background and added the numerous white dots to each of the line intersections. I took these few simple layers into Photoshop in order to add glowing effects around the prominent lines and illuminate the dots, as well as modify lightness values.

After an initial concept approval, I began to colorize the photo and realized the need for further value adjustment and more detail. I added an image of the Hubble Deep Field to the background, skewing it to fit along the four axes, giving the photo more depth. Using blending modes and transparency masks, I overlaid the images with a ruby-to-purple radial gradient. I then took an image of candle smoke and blended that atop the previous blend.

While adjusting color and opacity values, I realized an opportunity to add further detail to the geometric background in Adobe Illustrator. I repeatedly duplicated each square of the background—and shrunk the new subsequent square's dimensions by 10%—until each one was filled. I brought the new pattern into Photoshop and applied the same blending mode as I did to the rest of the geometric background.

To get a shimmering particle effect on all of the lines, I duplicated the glow of each object layer and set the blending mode to dissolve. With a few minor touch ups, the project is now in its current standing.
