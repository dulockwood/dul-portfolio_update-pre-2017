Live Your Truth

KENZIE KLINE | FREELANCE CLIENT
MARCH 2017 - PRESENT

Kenzie has a passion for inspiring the creativity of others. In her spare time, she manifests and illustrates with a variety of mediums.

To house her work and thoughts, she created a site utilizing Squarespace. I am making modifications to improve the user experience. This is my current progress in the project; recoding her site will proceed in late April.

Before || After

One convenient aspect of this project is the great starting concept. There already existed a clear typographical hierarchy and general responsive layout.

I am utilizing my UI Design experience to refine this base while maintaining a realistic expectation of what I will be able to execute in code. With only slight adjustments to alignment and element associations on this featured page, the user experience for a smartphone visitor is substantially improved.

First Step to Execution

Every project starts with an idea; something that can look so perfect as you first imagine it. But reality has a way of constraining that idea, unlike your mind. Sketching concepts is essential to giving yourself a realistic framework around which to design.

In this sketch, I iterate different phases of the new mobile nav bar. Toward the bottom of the image, I create a model for the relative z-indexes of elements around the nav.

Manifesting Animation Concepts

When inspiration strikes for a sleek motion solution to a visual/layout problem, I capture it to the best of my ability in a sketch. From there, I can determine how to most efficiently execute the concept in code.

Minor Adjustments for Major Results

While the default dropdown navigation style in Squarespace achieves basic functionality, its initial design does not serve mobile users as effectively as it could. When closed, it isn't as apparent that the nav can be triggered by both the word and the dropdown icon.

I adjusted the design in such a way that keeps things visually similar, but groups both these elements into one div that can be easily tapped by a finger. There is a clearer association between these elements as a result of being contained within the light-gray borders, and this container better separates the logo and slogan from the rest of the content on the page.

Even More Mobile-Friendly

As a mobile user scrolls down the page, the nav slowly moves upward and covers the fixed slogan utilizing z-indexes. Simultaneously, the slogan fades to 0% opacity while the logo inches upward and shrinks into its final position. The logo slightly overlaps the nav to give a subtle sense of depth.

Tapping the nav container brings up a fixed position list of other pages on the site. Content outside the nav will be muted by a slightly opaque div element. Retapping the highlighted nav container will close it. ["Home" will not likely exist in the final product; the logo itself will serve as a way to return to the proposed full-art landing page.]

For the rest of a site page, the nav and slogan will remain fixed in these positions. Scrolling up to the top of a page will rest them to their initial positions.

Calling To Action

The only place on Kenzie's site where users can subscribe to her email feed is on the "Home" page. Unfortunately, it isn't as clear to a user how often she intends to use this feature, and there is a chance that a user may skip right by it en route to the "Art!" or "Blog" page.

In the current proposed mock-up, I have moved this Call To Action into the footer of each site page, reinforcing its importance. The background color of the footer is heavily contrasted with the rest of the site as a way of drawing a user's attention to it.

Prime Screen Real Estate

When a user enters the site on a tablet, they should be served an experience that utilizes the larger screen; no reason to compact everything down, and no reason to stretch elements larger than they need to be.

The footer of each page will adapt to the screen size, splitting the content into two, equal-width columns. A darker color is used for the "Let's Get Creative" column in order to emphasize the intended hierarchy, as well as take advantage of the fact that many Western users browse a website as they would read a book: top to bottom, left to right.

Look Mom! Tablet Too!

Maintaining consistency of style across varying screen widths, while serving to conserve space. The tablet nav expands the list from the mobile menu across the dimensions of the screen.

As like on mobile, the slogan will fade to 0% opacity as it is covered by the nav. The logo will simultaneously transform to its final size and position.

Utilizing The Space

When you approach a website's structure and design from a mobile-first perspective, you learn what information is essential to provide, and what can be cut. As you design for larger and larger screens, you find creative ways of implementing the supplemental information that may have been cut beforehand.

On this blog landing page, the cards for each blog post include a supplemental image for tablet screens—excluded from mobile screens to save space.
